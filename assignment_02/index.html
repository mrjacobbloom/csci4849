Experiment Summary

Our team sought to explore the link between throughput and the act of either ascending or descending a staircase. We organized a test of throughput via the Fitts' Law experiment application GoFitts with 4 test subjects (the authors).

For each direction, subjects completed a pointing test that consisted of 12 "blocks" of 5 trials each. It was administered on a laptop, which was held in the subject's non-dominant hand while they completed the pointing tasks with their dominant hand. First, the subject would complete the tests while ascending the stairs. Then, they would complete the tests again while descending the stairs. We asked subjects to start each trial when they reached the second step of the staircase, and to try to complete each trial before they reached the landing.

The settings we used for the GoFitts trial are shown to the right.

Data & Analysis

Our tests showed evidence that mean throughput is consistently higher when ascending a staircase than descending. For test subjects 1 and 3, this only amounted to a difference of about 0.1 bits/sec. But for subject 4, the effect was more pronounced, with a difference of 0.4 bits/sec between their ascending and descending mean throughputs.

Further Experimentation

This experiment really begs more questions than it does provide answers. For example, does the size or length of the staircase have a meaningful effect on throughput? We had subjects try to complete each trial before they reached the landing, which likely affected their pace. A longer staircase (or better yet, a stair-stepper machine) might yield interestingly divergent results.

One variable that may have tainted our data is exhaustion. It's possible that, without allowing for cool-down time between the ascending and descending conditions, subjects were more winded during the descending part of the experiment.
